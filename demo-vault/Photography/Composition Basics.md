# Composition Basics

The handful of ideas that improved my photos more than any new lens did.

## Subtract, Don't Add

The most common fix is removing things, not adding them. Walk three steps left to get the trash can out of frame. Wait for the cluttered foreground to clear. A photo is as much about what you exclude as what you include.

## Where Things Go

- Rule of thirds is training wheels — useful until it's automatic, then break it.
- Put the horizon high for foreground interest, low for big skies. Dead center only on purpose.
- Leave room in the direction a subject is moving or looking. Crowding the edge feels wrong for a reason.

## Lines and Layers

- Leading lines pull the eye in: roads, fences, shadows, a row of windows.
- Foreground, middle, background gives a flat scene depth. Stick something close in the frame.
- Frames within frames — a doorway, an arch, branches — focus attention without a single word.

## Light Is The Subject

Composition arranges light as much as objects. Side light reveals texture, backlight makes outlines glow, flat overcast light is forgiving but dull. I'll move my whole position to put the light where it does work.

## The Background

I check the background before the subject now. A bright blob behind someone's head ruins a portrait faster than a soft focus does. Darker, simpler, further back — that's the rule.

## What I Stopped Doing

Centering everything out of habit, and shooting from standing eye level for every shot. Crouch, climb, get low. The angle nobody else bothered with is usually the photo.
