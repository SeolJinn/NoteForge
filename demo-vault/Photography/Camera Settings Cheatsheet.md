# Camera Settings Cheatsheet

The numbers I reach for so I'm not fumbling in the moment. Pairs with [[Travel Photography Tips]].

## The Triangle, Plainly

- **Aperture** controls how much is in focus. f/1.8 for one sharp eye and creamy background, f/8 for a landscape sharp front to back.
- **Shutter** controls motion. 1/500 freezes a running kid, 1/30 blurs a waterfall, 1/4 needs a tripod.
- **ISO** is the volume knob for light. Keep it low for clean files; raise it only when aperture and shutter have nothing left to give.

Change one, and you trade against the other two. That's the whole game.

## Starting Points

- **Daylight street:** aperture priority, f/5.6, auto-ISO, 1/250 minimum shutter.
- **Portrait:** f/2, shutter 1/200, ISO as low as it'll stay.
- **Landscape:** f/8 to f/11, base ISO, tripod, whatever shutter that needs.

## Low Light

This is where most shots fall apart, so I plan for it.

- Open the aperture all the way first. Free light, no quality cost.
- Drop the shutter to about 1/focal length handheld — 1/60 on a 50mm — and lean on something.
- Only then raise ISO. Modern sensors are clean to 3200, fine to 6400 in a pinch.
- A slightly noisy sharp shot beats a clean blurry one every time.

## Defaults I Keep

- RAW always. Exposure compensation -1/3 to protect highlights.
- Back-button focus, so focus and shutter aren't fighting.
- Auto-ISO with a ceiling set, so the camera can't surprise me at ISO 25600.

## What I Stopped Doing

Shooting full manual for everything to prove a point. Aperture priority with auto-ISO is faster and sharper in 90% of situations.
