# Meditation Notes

Almost a decade in, mostly off, occasionally on. What I've actually retained.

## The Practice

Twenty minutes, eyes closed, sitting upright (not lying down — I fall asleep). Attention on the breath at the nostrils. Notice when the mind wanders, return to the breath. That's it. Everything else is decoration.

## What It Isn't

- Not relaxation. Some sessions are restful; many are uncomfortable. The point isn't to feel good — the point is to notice clearly.
- Not emptying the mind. Thoughts arrive constantly. Noticing them and returning is the practice. You don't fail by thinking; you only fail by not noticing.
- Not religion. The Buddhist framing is the most articulate but the technique stands alone.

## What's Changed for Me

- A small gap between stimulus and reaction. Not always, but more often than before.
- A different relationship to anxiety. The anxiety still arrives; I'm less likely to mistake it for a fact about the world.
- Better sleep onset. Possibly the biggest concrete benefit.

## What Hasn't Changed

- I am not calmer day-to-day in any obvious way.
- Stressful situations still feel stressful in the moment.
- I still get angry. I still procrastinate. The practice is not a personality transplant.

## On Apps

Used Headspace, then Waking Up, now nothing. The apps are useful as scaffolding for the first six months. After that they become entertainment.

## On Retreats

Did one ten-day silent retreat. Punishing, valuable, would do again — but not soon. The afterglow lasted six months.

## The Real Lesson

The practice doesn't work because it's profound. It works because it's repetitive. Twenty minutes a day for years is the only mechanism. Skipping is fine. Quitting is the only failure.
